Fluoxetine is a type of antidepressant known as an SSRI (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor).

It is often used to treat depression, and also sometimes obsessive compulsive disorder.

Fluoxetine helps many people recover from depression, and it has fewer unwanted effects than older antidepressants.

It is also called Prozac.

Key facts

  • It usually takes 4-6 weeks to feel the full benefits of fluoxetine.
  • Common side effects include feeling sick, headache and trouble sleeping, but tend to be mild and pass after a couple of weeks.
  • Fluoxetine can affect an unborn baby. Tell your doctor straight away if you’re trying to get pregnant or become pregnant while taking it.
  • Fluoxetine can cause withdrawal symptoms so don’t stop taking it without talking to your doctor.
  • You can drink alcohol while taking fluoxetine but it might make you feel sleepy.

How and when to take

Take fluoxetine once a day. You can choose to take it at any time, as long as you stick to the same time every day. You can take fluoxetine with or without food.

The usual dose of fluoxetine in adults is 20mg daily.

If you are taking fluoxetine as a liquid medicine, a 5ml spoonful is the same as taking one 20mg capsule.

More information

Fluoxetine can be used by children aged 8 or older to treat moderate or severe depression.

The usual dose in children is 10mg daily, but your child’s doctor might increase this to 20mg if needed.

What if I forget to take it?

If you forget to take a dose, don’t worry. Take your next dose the next day at the usual time.

What if I take too much?

Ask your doctor for advice straight away as overdose can lead to potentially serious symptoms such as:

  • vomiting
  • tremor
  • feeling sleepy
  • agitation
  • heart problems
  • lung problems
  • seizures

Never take two doses at the same time, or take extra doses.

Will my dose go up or down?

The usual dose of fluoxetine is 20mg daily in adults. However, it may be started at a lower dose and may be gradually increased to a maximum dose of 60mg daily.

Some people might need to take a lower dose of fluoxetine, or to take it less often. This includes people with liver problems, and elderly people.

The usual dose of fluoxetine in children aged 8 or older is 10mg daily, but this may be increased to 20mg daily.

Side effects

Not everyone will get side effects with fluoxetine, but common ones include feeling sick, headaches, sleep problems, feeling tired, and diarrhoea.

More information

Common side effects

Like all medicines, fluoxetine can cause side effects in some people, but many people have no side effects or only minor ones.

These very common side effects affect more than one in 10 people. Keep taking the medicine, but tell your doctor if they bother you or don't go away:

  • feeling sick
  • headache
  • being unable to sleep
  • diarrhoea
  • feeling tired or weak

Serious side effects

It happens rarely, but some people may have serious side effects when taking fluoxetine.

Tell a doctor straight away if you develop:

  • serotonin syndrome: warning signs include dizziness, very severe headache, agitation, hallucinations, fever, a fast or unusual heartbeat, flushing, seizures, shakiness, sweating a lot, change in thinking clearly, very upset stomach and vomiting, or severe diarrhoea
  • low sodium levels: warning signs include headache, trouble focusing, memory problems, not thinking clearly, weakness, seizures, or losing your balance
  • thoughts about harming yourself or ending your life
  • chest pain or pressure or shortness of breath
  • severe dizziness or passing out
  • fits, feelings of euphoria, excessive enthusiasm or excitement, or a feeling of restlessness that means you can’t sit or stand still
  • putting on or losing weight without trying to
  • changes in your periods such as heavy bleeding, spotting, or bleeding between periods
  • painful erections that last longer than four hours - this may happen even when you are not having sex

Or, if you get any signs of abnormal bleeding including:

  • bleeding from the gut: warning signs include vomiting blood or dark vomit; coughing up blood; blood in your urine; black or red poo
  • bleeding from the gums or bruises that appear without a reason or that get bigger
  • any bleeding that is very bad or that you cannot stop

Serious allergic reaction

It’s rare to have an allergic reaction to fluoxetine but if this happens to you, stop taking the medicine and contact a doctor straight away.

The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

  • getting a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
  • wheezing
  • tightness in the chest or throat
  • having trouble breathing or talking
  • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat
Info:

These are not all the side effects of fluoxetine. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.

Coping with side effects

Some of the common side effects of fluoxetine will gradually improve as your body gets used to it.

  • What to do about feeling sick or indigestion: take fluoxetine with food and plenty of water.
  • What to do about feeling sleepy: avoid driving or using tools or machinery until the sleepiness wears off. Reduce the amount of alcohol you drink.
  • What to do if you’re unable to sleep: take fluoxetine first thing in the morning.

You can lessen the chance of having a side effect if you take fluoxetine in the evening so that you're asleep when the level of medicine in your body is highest.

If you have diabetes fluoxetine can upset its control. Monitor your blood glucose more often for the first few weeks of treatment with fluoxetine and adjust your diabetes treatment if necessary.

If you have a side effect from any medicine, including fluoxetine, you can report it using the Yellow Card Scheme.

Who may not be able to take fluoxetine

Check with your doctor before starting to take fluoxetine if you:

  • have an allergy to fluoxetine or any of its ingredients
  • are taking any other medicines, including any complementary medicines. Fluoxetine may not be suitable for you if you have a heart condition or take medicines that affect your heartbeat
  • have ever taken any other medicines for depression. Some rarely used antidepressants can interact with fluoxetine to cause very high blood pressure even when they have been stopped for a few weeks
  • are trying to become pregnant, are already pregnant or are breastfeeding

Common questions

Is fluoxetine better than other antidepressants?

Fluoxetine isn't any better or worse than other antidepressants. However, sometimes people respond better to one antidepressant than to another. Talk to your doctor if you aren’t feeling any better after 6 weeks.

Can I drink alcohol?

You can drink alcohol while taking fluoxetine but you might find that it makes you more sleepy, or makes you lose your focus. During the first few days of taking fluoxetine, it might be best to stop drinking alcohol until you see how the medicine affects you.

Will I gain or lose weight?

Fluoxetine can make you feel less hungry than usual, so you may lose weight when you start taking it.

If you start to have problems with your weight while taking fluoxetine, talk to your doctor or pharmacist.

Will it affect my sex life?

The good effects of fluoxetine may, after a while, enhance your sex life as your mood lifts and you become interested in life and relationships again.

Some of the possible negative effects include:

  • men might get painful erections, problems with getting an erection and problems with ejaculating
  • women might have some vaginal bleeding and might not reach orgasm the same way as before
  • you may have a lower sex drive

Sexual side effects should pass after the first couple of weeks. If they don’t, and this causes a problem for you, go back to your doctor to see if there’s an another antidepressant for you to try.

Will street drugs affect it?

It can be potentially dangerous (even fatal) to take fluoxetine with:

  • stimulants like MDMA (ecstasy) or cocaine
  • hallucinogens like LSD
  • some legal highs like mephedrone

The sleep-inducing effects of cannabis can add to those of fluoxetine, especially in people who have just started taking it.

Methadone can increase the risk of side effects in people taking fluoxetine.

Fluoxetine has not been properly tested with street drugs so tell your doctor if you think you might use any street drugs while taking fluoxetine.

Can I drive or ride a bike?

Most people drive as normal while taking fluoxetine. However, taking fluoxetine might affect your concentration to do things like driving a car, riding a bike or anything else that needs a lot of focus. It might be best to stop doing these things for the first few days until you know how it affects you.

Will it affect my contraception?

For women, fluoxetine will not affect contraceptive pills or the morning after pill.

Taking fluoxetine with other medicines

Tell your doctor and pharmacist that you are taking fluoxetine before starting or stopping any other prescription or one you buy from a pharmacy or shop.

More information

Mixing fluoxetine with complementary remedies

Don't take the herbal remedy for depression, St John's wort, at the same time as fluoxetine as this will increase your risk of side effects.

Tell your doctor and pharmacist that you’re taking fluoxetine before starting any complementary medicine.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

No antidepressants are considered completely safe to take in pregnancy or while breastfeeding, but your doctor will want to prescribe fluoxetine when the benefits to you of taking the medicine outweigh the risks.

More information

Taking fluoxetine in pregnancy

Fluoxetine and similar antidepressants have been linked with a small risk of problems for the unborn baby when they're taken in early or late pregnancy.

Tell your doctor if you’re trying to get pregnant or become pregnant while taking this medicine.

Fluoxetine and breastfeeding

Fluoxetine passes into breast milk and has been linked with side effects in breastfed babies.

Talk to your doctor, as there may be other antidepressants that are more suitable for you while you are breastfeeding.

More about fluoxetine

How fluoxetine works

Fluoxetine is one of a group of antidepressants called selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, or SSRIs, which are thought to work by increasing the levels of a mood-enhancing chemical called serotonin in the brain.

When will I feel better?

You may see an improvement in your symptoms after a week or two although it usually takes between 4 and 6 weeks before you feel the full benefits. That’s because it takes around a week for fluoxetine levels to build up in your body, and then a few weeks longer for your body to adapt and get used to it.

Don't stop taking fluoxetine after a week or two just because you feel it is not helping your symptoms. Give the medicine at least 6 weeks to work.

How long will I take fluoxetine for?

Once your condition is under control it is likely that you will continue to take fluoxetine for several more months to prevent the symptoms returning.

You will need to discuss with your doctor the pros and cons of continuing to take fluoxetine in the long term. The decision will depend on the type and severity of your initial symptoms, whether it's a one-off or recurring problem, how well fluoxetine works for you, and whether you have had any serious side effects.

What else can fluoxetine be used for?

Fluoxetine is mainly used to treat depression, obsessive compulsive disorder, and bulimia. It is occasionally used to treat body dysmorphic disorder, and depression caused by bipolar disorder.

Non-medical treatment options

Antidepressants, including fluoxetine, are just one of several approaches to treating depression. Other potential treatments include:

  • talking therapy (such as cognitive behavioural therapy)
  • exercise programmes
  • help to get a good night's sleep

Choosing a treatment that's most suitable for you depends on how long you have had depression, your symptoms, whether you have had any previous bouts of depression, whether previous treatment has worked, how likely you are to stick with your treatment, the potential side effects and your preferences and priorities.

Coming off fluoxetine

Don’t stop taking fluoxetine unless your doctor tells you to.

If you stop taking fluoxetine suddenly you may get unpleasant withdrawal symptoms. These include:

  • dizziness
  • nausea
  • numbness or tingling in the hands or feet
  • trouble sleeping
  • feeling agitated or anxious
  • headaches
  • shaking

Withdrawal symptoms are usually harmless and over within a few days but it's possible to prevent them by reducing the dose gradually when you stop taking this medicine.
