Simvastatin belongs to a group of medicines called statins. It’s often used to lower the levels of cholesterol and other fatty substances (triglycerides) in the blood.

It is also taken by people who have had a heart attack or who have unstable angina to reduce the risk of another heart attack or stroke.

If you are at high risk of developing cardiovascular disease, for example, because you have diabetes, simvastatin also helps reduce this risk.

Simvastatin can be taken by children over the age of 10.

The medicine is available on prescription as tablets.

Simvastatin is also called by the brand names of Zocor and Simvador.

Key facts

  • Simvastatin works better if you take it in the evening.
  • It's a very safe medicine with few side effects.
  • Simvastatin can harm a baby. Tell your doctor if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant or breastfeeding.
  • Some medicines do not mix well with simvastatin. Talk to your doctor or pharmacist before starting any medicines including herbal remedies and supplements.
  • Don’t drink grapefruit juice while taking simvastatin. It increases the chances of side effects.
Info:

Check the leaflet inside your medicine packet for specific information about the brand that you are taking.

How and when to take

Take simvastatin once a day in the evening.

Simvastatin doesn't upset the stomach so you can take it with or without food.

The usual dose is between 10mg and 40mg once a day.

More information

Swallow simvastatin tablets whole with a glass of water. Simvastatin works better at lowering cholesterol if you take it in the evening rather than in the morning.

How much will I take?

Your dose depends on the reason for taking it, your cholesterol levels and what other medicines you are taking.

In adults, the usual dose is 10mg to 40mg once a day. An 80mg per day dose may be prescribed for people with very high cholesterol levels.

The usual starting dose in children is 10mg each evening but your child’s doctor might increase this after four weeks. The maximum dose in children is 40mg.

What if I forget to take it?

If you forget to take a dose, take your next dose the next day at the usual time. Never take two doses at the same time or extra doses.

What if I take too much?

Accidentally taking an extra dose of simvastatin is unlikely to harm you.

Speak to your pharmacist or doctor if you are worried, or take more than one extra dose.

Side effects

Simvastatin seems to be a very safe medicine. It's rare to have side effects with it.

One rare but serious side effect is unexplained muscle pain, tenderness or weakness. This is more likely if you are taking a higher dose of simvastatin, particularly the 80mg dose.

Report any unexplained muscle pain, tenderness or weakness to a doctor straight away.

More information

Serious side effects

It happens rarely, but up to one in 1000 people taking simvastatin may have a serious side effect.

Stop taking simvastatin and tell a doctor or go to hospital straight away if you develop:

  • muscle pain, tenderness, weakness or cramps (a sign of muscle breakdown and kidney damage)
  • liver problems: warning signs include yellowing of the skin and eyes, dark-coloured urine or pale coloured poo, itching, feeling tired or weak and loss of appetite
  • severe tummy pain (a sign of inflammation of the pancreas)
  • cough, feeling short of breath and weight loss (signs of interstitial lung disease)
Info:

These are not all the side effects of simvastatin. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.

Serious allergic reaction

It's rare to have an allergic reaction to simvastatin but if this happens to you, stop taking the medicine and contact a doctor straight away.

The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

  • a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
  • wheezing
  • tightness in the chest or throat
  • trouble breathing or talking
  • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat
Info:

If you have a side effect from any medicine, including simvastatin, you can report it using the Yellow Card Scheme.

Who can take simvastatin

Simvastatin isn’t suitable for some people.

Tell your doctor if you:

  • have an allergy to simvastatin
  • have liver or kidney problems
  • are trying to become pregnant, are already pregnant or breastfeeding
  • have severe lung disease
  • regularly drink large amounts of alcohol (more than two glasses a day)
  • have an underactive thyroid
  • have experienced muscular side effects when taking a statin
  • (or a close relative) have had a muscle disorder

Common questions

Can I drink alcohol?

Yes, you can drink alcohol while taking simvastatin.

However, drinking a lot of alcohol may increase the risk of muscle and liver side effects. Try not to drink more than two glasses of alcohol a day.

How long will I take simvastatin for?

In most cases, treatment with a statin such as simvastatin is for life. The benefits will only continue for as long as you take it. If you stop taking simvastatin without starting an alternative treatment your cholesterol level may rise again.

Can I drive while taking simvastatin?

Most people can drive as normal while taking simvastatin. Simvastatin can cause dizziness rarely in up to one in 1000 people.

Do not drive or use machines or tools if you are suffering from dizziness.

Will it affect my contraception?

For women, simvastatin will not affect contraceptive pills or the morning after pill.

Will it affect my fertility?

There is no evidence atorvastatin will reduce your fertility.

Is this medicine addictive?

No, there is no evidence that simvastatin is addictive.

Taking simvastatin with other medicines

There are some medicines that do not mix well with simvastatin. They can change the way it works or increase the risk of serious side effects.

Simvastatin can also change the way other medicines work.

Tell your doctor or pharmacist if you are taking any other medicines, including herbal remedies and supplements, before starting simvastatin.

More information

Medicines that may not mix well with simvastatin include:

  • some antibiotics and antifungals
  • some HIV medicines
  • some hepatitis C medicines
  • warfarin (stops blood clotting)
  • ciclosporin (treats psoriasis and rheumatoid arthritis)
  • danazol (treats endometriosis)
  • nefazodone (treats depression)
  • amiodarone (makes your heart stable)
  • verapamil, diltiazem, amlodipine (for high blood pressure and heart conditions)

If you are taking simvastatin and need to take one of these medicines, your doctor may:

  • prescribe a lower dose of simvastatin
  • prescribe a different statin medicine
  • recommend that you temporarily stop taking your simvastatin

Mixing simvastatin with complementary remedies

St John’s Wort, a herbal medicine taken for depression, reduces the amount of simvastatin in your blood so it doesn't work as well.

Talk to your doctor before starting St John’s Wort, as you may need a higher dose of simvastatin.

Tell your doctor and pharmacist that you are taking simvastatin before starting or stopping any complementary medicine.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

Do not take simvastatin if you are pregnant or breastfeeding.

If you become pregnant while taking simvastatin, stop taking the medicine and tell your doctor.

Tell your doctor if you plan to get pregnant, become pregnant, or wish to breastfeed whilst taking this medicine.

More information

It's not recommended that women take simvastatin in pregnancy as there is no firm evidence that it's safe.

Talk to your doctor if you are planning to become pregnant. It's best to stop taking simvastatin at least three months before you start trying for a baby.

Simvastatin and breastfeeding

It’s not known if simvastatin passes into breast milk but it may cause problems for your baby.

Talk to your doctor if you want to breastfeed. You may be able to stop taking simvastatin temporarily while you breastfeed.

More about simvastatin

How simvastatin works

Simvastatin acts on the liver to stop it making cholesterol. This lowers your overall blood cholesterol level.

Are there other cholesterol-lowering medicines?

There are several other 'statin' medicines including:

  • atorvastatin
  • fluvastatin
  • pravastatin
  • rosuvastatin

Statins all work in the same way, but they differ in how well they lower cholesterol.

If you have a side effect with one statin, this may not happen with another. And some medicines don’t mix well with one statin but you can take them with a different one.

Your doctor will find the right statin and dose for you, depending on your medical history, cholesterol level and the other medicines you take.

Food and drinks to avoid

Don’t drink grapefruit juice if you are taking simvastatin.

Grapefruit juice increases the concentration of simvastatin in your blood which makes side effects more likely.

Coming off simvastatin

Talk to your doctor if you stop or want to stop taking simvastatin.

You may want to stop simvastatin if you think you are having side effects. Talk to your doctor to see if it really is a side effect of simvastatin or another problem. It may help to lower your dose or change your medicine.

Stopping simvastatin may cause your cholesterol to rise. If you want to stop taking the medicine, it’s important to find another way to lower your cholesterol.

Non-medical treatment options

There are lifestyle changes you can make that will help lower your cholesterol. Reducing your cholesterol may mean you don't need to be prescribed a statin medicine.

  • Lose excess weight.
  • Eat heart-healthy foods by decreasing saturated fat and increasing fibre, fruits, vegetables, whole grains and fish.
  • Stop smoking.
  • Reduce the amount of alcohol you drink.
  • Exercise every day

As part of a healthy diet, it can also help to include foods fortified with plant sterols and stanols which are proven to help lower cholesterol. You can buy spreads, cheese, cereals, granola bars, orange juice, wholemeal bread and yoghurt-style drinks which have been fortified with plant sterols and stanols from most supermarkets.
