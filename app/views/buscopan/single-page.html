Buscopan is an antispasmodic medicine often used to relieve painful tummy cramps and irritable bowel syndrome (IBS).

Buscopan contains the active ingredient, hyoscine butylbromide. It’s not the same as hyoscine hydrobromide, which is a different medicine taken to prevent travel sickness.

Buscopan comes as tablets which are available on prescription and to buy from pharmacies. It can also be given by injection but this is usually only done in hospital.

The medicine is also called by brand names Buscopan IBS Relief and Buscopan Cramps.

It can be taken by children aged six and over.

Key facts

  • Buscopan relieves the pain of tummy cramps by helping your digestive system to relax.
  • The medicine works very quickly - you can expect painful cramps to ease within 15 minutes.
  • It's unusual to have any side effects but some people get a dry mouth, constipation and blurred vision.
  • Most people take Buscopan for a few days only, but you can safely take it for many months if your doctor has said it's ok to do so.
Info:

Check the leaflet inside your medicine packet for specific information about the brand that you are taking.

How much to take

Take Buscopan tablets as and when you have painful tummy cramps.

Each tablet contains 10mg of hyoscine butylbromide.

The usual dose for adults and children aged six years and over is 1 or 2 tablets taken three or four times a day.

Buscopan doesn't upset the stomach so you can take it with or without food.

More information

The usual starting dose in adults and children aged 12 years and over is one tablet taken three times a day. If you need more relief, you can increase the dose to a maximum of two tablets taken four times a day.

In children aged between 6 and 11 years old, the usual dose is one tablet taken three times a day.

Do not give Buscopan to a child younger than six.

What if I forget to take it?

If you forget to take a dose, take it as soon as you remember. If you don't remember until it is time for the next dose, skip the missed dose.

Never take two doses at the same time or an extra dose to make up for a forgotten one.

What if I take too much?

Accidentally taking an extra dose of Buscopan is unlikely to harm you.

Speak to your pharmacist or doctor if you are worried, or take more than one extra dose.

Side effects

Not many people get side effects with Buscopan. If they do happen, the most common side effects are dry mouth, constipation, blurred vision and a faster heartbeat.

More information

Like all medicines, Buscopan can cause side effects in some people but most people have no side effects or only minor ones.

These side effects happen in up to one in 100 people.

Tell your doctor or pharmacist if they bother you or don't go away:

  • dry mouth
  • constipation
  • blurred vision
  • fast heart rate

Serious side effects

It happens rarely, but some people may have a serious side effect when taking Buscopan.

Stop taking Buscopan and tell a doctor straight away if you:

  • get a painful red eye with loss of vision (this could be caused by an increase in pressure in your eye)
  • find it hard to pee
Info:

These are not all the side effects of Buscopan. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.

Serious allergic reaction

It's rare to have an allergic reaction to Buscopan but if this happens to you, stop taking the medicine and contact a doctor straight away.

The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

  • getting a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
  • wheezing
  • tightness in the chest or throat
  • having trouble breathing or talking
  • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat
Info:

If you have a side effect from any medicine, including Buscopan, you can report it using the Yellow Card Scheme.

Who may not be able to take Buscopan

Buscopan may not be suitable for everyone.

To make sure it is safe for you, tell your doctor or pharmacist before starting Buscopan if you have:

  • an allergy to the active ingredient, hyoscine butylbromide
  • a rare type of glaucoma called angle-closure glaucoma (Buscopan can increase the pressure in the eye and make your glaucoma worse)
  • a very enlarged bowel
  • myasthenia gravis (an illness where the muscles become weak and tire easily)

Buscopan tablets are not usually recommended for children under the age of six.

More information

Check with your doctor or pharmacist before taking Buscopan if you:

  • are trying for a baby, are pregnant or you are breastfeeding
  • cannot digest some sugars (as Buscopan tablets contain sugars)
  • have a very fast heart rate or any other heart problems (Buscopan tablets may make the heart beat speed up even more)
  • have difficulty peeing, for example men with prostate problems
  • have a gut blockage
  • have an overactive thyroid
  • have a temperature above 38C
  • have a digestive problems such as reflux disease, severe constipation or ulcerative colitis

There are some extra safety checks to think about before you take Buscopan Cramps and Buscopan IBS relief.

For safety, talk to your doctor or pharmacist before taking Buscopan Cramps and Buscopan IBS if you:

  • are 40 years or over
  • have recently passed blood in your poo
  • are feeling sick or vomiting
  • have lost your appetite or lost weight
  • are looking pale and feeling tired
  • have unusual vaginal bleeding or vaginal discharge
  • have recently travelled abroad (in case you have picked up a stomach bug)

Only take Buscopan IBS relief tablets if your doctor has said that you have irritable bowel syndrome.

Common questions

When will I feel better?

Buscopan tablets start to work within 15 minutes. Talk to your pharmacist or doctor if your symptoms change, get worse or do not get better within two weeks.

Can I drink alcohol?

Yes, you can drink alcohol with Buscopan.

Can I drive while taking Buscopan?

Buscopan can cause blurred vision or dizziness in some people. If this happens to you, don't drive or use machinery or tools until it's worn off.

Are there other IBS remedies?

There are other IBS remedies including peppermint oil (Colpermin) and mebeverine (Colofac).

Peppermint oil and mebeverine are also antispasmodic remedies. They work in a similar way to Buscopan to relax tummy muscles and ease painful cramps.

Both peppermint oil and mebeverine are available to buy from high street pharmacies. Mebeverine is also available on prescription.

Can I take Buscopan with other IBS remedies?

Usually it's best not to take Buscopan at the same time as peppermint oil or mebeverine – just take one or the other.

Can I take Buscopan with painkillers?

Yes, you can take Buscopan at the same time as everyday painkillers like paracetamol and ibuprofen.

Taking Buscopan with other medicines

Some medicines and Buscopan can interfere with each other and increase the chances of you having side effects.

Tell your doctor or pharmacist if you are taking any other medicines, including herbal remedies and supplements, before starting Buscopan.

More information

If you take Buscopan at the same time as some travel sickness medicines, such as domperidone and metoclopramide, they can cancel each other out and stop each other from working. This is because metoclopramide and domperidone increase the movement of the gut, whereas Buscopan reduces it.

Some medicines increase the risk of side effects if they're taken at the same time as Buscopan including:

  • codeine
  • medicines to treat allergies such as antihistamines
  • some antidepressants such as amitriptyline 
  • some medicines for mental health problems like schizophrenia or bipolar disorder such as clozapine or chlorpromazine
  • amantadine (taken for Parkinson’s disease)
  • quinidine or disopyramide (heart medicines)
  • some inhaled asthma medicines including salbutamol, iptratropium, tiotropium

Mixing Buscopan with complementary remedies

There are no known problems with taking herbal and complementary remedies at the same time as Buscopan.

For safety, speak to your pharmacist before taking Buscopan if you routinely take any herbal or alternative remedies.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

Buscopan isn't usually recommended during pregnancy and while breastfeeding.

Talk to your doctor before taking Buscopan if you’re trying to become pregnant, are pregnant or you are breastfeeding.

More information

Buscopan hasn't been fully tested in pregnant women so it's not possible to say it's absolutely safe to take in pregnancy.

Talk to your doctor about the pros and cons of taking this medicine in pregnancy.

Buscopan and breastfeeding

Tiny amounts of Buscopan may get into breast milk, but it’s not known whether it harms the baby.

There is a small risk that Buscopan may decrease your milk production.

Talk to your doctor about the pros and cons of taking this medicine while you are breastfeeding.

More about Buscopan

How Buscopan works

Buscopan relieves the pain of tummy cramps by helping your digestive system to relax.

It does this by reducing the wave-like contractions of the muscle in the walls of the stomach and bowel.

Buscopan helps to treat tummy cramps but does not cure them.

The difference between Buscopan products

Buscopan Cramps and Buscopan IBS Relief both contain the same active ingredient, hyoscine butylbromide 10mg, but they are for different medical problems.

Buscopan Cramps is meant for general tummy cramps and for the symptoms of irritable bowel syndrome (IBS). It can be taken by adults and children 6 years old and over.

Buscopan IBS Relief is meant only for IBS that has been confirmed by a doctor. It can be taken by adults and children 12 years old and over.

Taking Buscopan for a long time

It’s safe to take Buscopan for as long as you need to provided that your doctor has said that it’s ok and you aren't having any side effects.

Buscopan seems to be a very safe medicine. It's been available for more than 50 years and there haven’t been any problems in people taking it, even for many months.

Non-medical treatment options

There are a number of lifestyle changes you can make to prevent or ease painful tummy cramps and IBS.

It can really help to understand which, if any, foods trigger your cramps and then reduce or remove them from your diet. Common triggers include caffeine, fizzy drinks and fried food. 

It can also help to control your stress levels, for example by doing breathing exercises or trying a relaxation therapy such as Yoga, pilates or meditation.

And aim to exercise three times a week for at least 30 minutes - regular activity is good for your digestive system.
