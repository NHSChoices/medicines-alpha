Gabapentin for migraine

Gabapentin is a type of painkiller that is sometimes used to prevent migraine headaches.

Gabapentin is usually only taken by adults.

It is available only on prescription as capsules, tablets and as a liquid that you swallow.

The medicine is also called by various brand names, including Neurontin.

Key facts

  • Gabapentin is usually taken three times a day. You can take it with or without food.
  • Gabapentin can cause withdrawal problems. Don’t stop the medicine suddenly unless your doctor asks you to.
  • The most common side effects of gabapentin are feeling sleepy, tiredness and dizziness.
  • You can drink alcohol while taking gabapentin, but it may make you feel sleepy or tired.
Info:

Check the leaflet inside your medicine packet for specific information about the brand that you are taking.

How and when to take

Most people need to take gabapentin three times a day.

Try to space your doses evenly through the day. For example, first thing in the morning, early afternoon and at bedtime.

Gabapentin doesn’t upset the stomach so you can take it with or without food, but it’s best to be consistent day after day.

More information

To avoid unwanted side effects, your doctor will prescribe a low dose to start with and then increase it over a few days.

Swallow gabapentin capsules and tablets with a drink of water or juice. Do not chew them.

Gabapentin tablets and capsules come in different strengths. Depending on your dose, you may need to split the tablets in half.

If you break a tablet and take only half of it, take the other half at your next dose. Always use a broken tablet as soon as possible, or within a few days. Never chew a broken tablet.

Only ever break tablets in half – capsules need to be swallowed whole.

Gabapentin is also available as a liquid for children and people who find it difficult to swallow tablets.

If you are taking gabapentin as a liquid, it will come with a dose-measuring plastic syringe or spoon to help you take the right amount. If you don’t have a dose-measuring device, ask your pharmacist for one.

How much will I take?

The usual dose to prevent migraines varies but can be up to 2,400mg a day.

How long will I take it for?

It is likely that once your migraines have gone you will continue to take gabapentin for several months to stop them coming back.

What if I forget to take it?

If you forget a dose, take it as soon as you remember. If it is within two hours of the next dose, it is better to leave out the missed dose and take your next dose as normal.

Never take two doses at the same time or an extra dose to make up for a forgotten one.

What if I take too much?

Accidentally taking an extra dose of gabapentin may cause unpleasant side effects. These include feeling dizzy or sleepy, seeing double, slurring your words, diarrhoea and passing out.

If this happens, call your doctor or NHS 111 straight away, or go to the nearest hospital accident and emergency department.

Take with you any gabapentin that you have left, and the labelled container so the hospital can see what you have taken.

Side effects

Most people who take gabapentin don’t have too much trouble with side effects. The most common ones are feeling sleepy, tiredness and dizziness. These tend to pass within a week or two as your body gets used to gabapentin.

More information

Common side effects

These very common side effects may happen in more than one in 10 people. They are usually mild and go away by themselves. Keep taking the medicine, but tell your doctor if they bother you or don't go away:

  • feeling sleepy, tired or dizzy
  • losing your coordination
  • feeling sick and vomiting
  • wind, diarrhoea and constipation (can’t poo)
  • indigestion
  • dry mouth

You may find you put on weight because gabapentin can make you feel hungry.

Sometimes the medicine can cause memory problems, mood changes, swollen arms and legs, blurred vision and, for men, difficulties with getting an erection.

Serious side effects

Very few people taking gabapentin have serious problems. It seems to be a very safe medicine.

Tell a doctor straight away if you notice a serious side effect. These are:

  • thoughts of harming or killing yourself. A small number of people taking gabapentin have had suicidal thoughts, and they can happen after only a week of treatment
  • yellowing of your skin or whites of your eyes. These may be warning signs of jaundice
  • unusual bruises or bleeding. These may be warning signs of a blood disorder
  • long-lasting stomach pain, feeling sick or being sick. These may be warning signs of an inflamed pancreas
  • muscle pain or weakness and you are on haemodialysis because of kidney failure.

Serious allergic reaction

It's extremely rare to have an allergic reaction to gabapentin but if this happens to you, stop taking the medicine and tell a doctor straight away.

The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

  • an itchy red rash
  • wheezing
  • tightness in the chest or throat
  • having trouble breathing or talking
  • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat

Serious allergic reaction is a medical emergency. Contact a doctor straight away if you think you or someone around you is having a serious allergic reaction.

Info:

These are not all the side effects of gabapentin. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.

How to cope with side effects

What to do about feeling sleepy, tired or dizzy

Don’t drive or use tools or machinery if you are feeling this way.

As your body gets used to gabapentin, these side effects should wear off.

If they don’t wear off within a week or two, your doctor may reduce your dose or increase it more slowly. If that doesn’t work you may need to switch to a different medicine.

What to do about feeling sick

Take gabapentin with or after a meal or snack to ease feelings of sickness. It may also help if you don’t eat rich or spicy food while you're taking this medicine.

What to do about diarrhoea and vomiting

Have small but frequent sips of water if you have diarrhoea or you are vomiting. It may also help to take oral rehydration solutions which you can buy from a pharmacy or supermarket to prevent dehydration.

Don’t take any other medicines to treat diarrhoea or vomiting without speaking to a pharmacist or doctor.

What to do about constipation

If you have constipation, eat more high-fibre foods such as fresh fruit and vegetables and cereals, and try to drink several glasses of water or other fluid each day. It may also help to exercise more regularly, for example, by going for a daily walk or run.

What to do about weight gain

Gabapentin can make you hungrier so it can be quite a challenge to stop yourself putting on weight.

Try to eat a healthy balanced diet without increasing your portion sizes. Don’t snack on foods that contain a lot of calories, such as crisps, cakes, biscuits and sweets.

If you feel hungry between meals, eat fruit and vegetables and low-calorie foods.

Regular exercise will also help to keep your weight stable.

What to do about a dry mouth

Try chewing sugar-free gum or sucking sugar-free sweets.

Taking gabapentin with other medicines

There aren’t usually any problems with mixing gabapentin with other medicines.

Gabapentin does not change the way other medicines work. And there are very few medicines that change the way gabapentin works.

Some indigestion remedies, called antacids, reduce the amount of gabapentin that the body takes in so it doesn’t work as well. This problem can be prevented if you take an antacid at least 2 hours before or after your dose of gabapentin.

Tell your doctor or pharmacist if you’re taking any other prescription medicines or a medicine you buy including herbal remedies and supplements before starting gabapentin.

More information

For safety, tell your doctor if you’re taking these medicines before you start gabapentin treatment:

  • strong painkillers such as morphine. These painkillers may increase the tiredness and dizziness you can feel when you start gabapentin
  • antidepressants such as amitriptyline or fluoxetine
  • antipsychotic medicines for mental health problems like schizophrenia or bipolar disorder
  • a medicine to prevent malaria called mefloquine
  • a weight loss medicine called Orlistat. It may stop gabapentin working as well

Mixing gabapentin with complementary remedies

There are no known problems with taking herbal and complementary remedies with gabapentin.

For safety, speak to your pharmacist before taking gabapentin if you routinely take any herbal or alternative remedies.

Common questions

Can I drink alcohol with gabapentin?

Yes, you can drink alcohol with gabapentin. However, it may make you feel sleepy or tired.

Can I drive or ride a bike with gabapentin?

You may feel sleepy, tired or dizzy when you first start taking gabapentin or if your dose has increased. Be very careful when you drive or ride a bike until you feel better.

When will I feel better?

It takes at least a few weeks for gabapentin to work properly. You may still have migraines during this time.

Are there any similar medicines?

Pregabalin (also called Lyrica) is a medicine that works in a similar way to gabapentin.

However, there are differences between pregabalin and gabapentin. Pregabalin can be taken less often and in different doses to gabapentin.

If you need to change to pregabalin treatment, your doctor will explain how to safely swap from gabapentin.

Should I stick to the same brand of gabapentin?

Most people don’t have to stay on the same brand of gabapentin as there is very little difference between brands. However, talk to your doctor if you are worried about switching to a different brand.

Will it stop my contraception working?

No, gabapentin doesn’t stop contraceptive pills, or any other type of contraceptive, from working.

Will it reduce my fertility?

No, gabapentin does not reduce fertility.

Who can take gabapentin

Gabapentin capsules contain gelatin so they are not suitable for vegetarians and vegans.

Some brands of gabapentin capsules contain small amounts of lactose, so they may be unsuitable for people with a digestive problem called lactose intolerance.

Gabapentin tablets and liquid do not contain gelatin or lactose.

Gabapentin liquid contains sodium and potassium. If you are on a controlled sodium or potassium diet, or your kidneys don’t work well, speak to your doctor before taking this liquid.

More information

Gabapentin isn't suitable for some people.

To make sure gabapentin is safe for you, tell your doctor if you:

  • have ever had an allergic reaction to gabapentin or another medicine
  • have kidney problems
  • have ever abused or been addicted to a medicine
  • are trying to get pregnant, are already pregnant or are breastfeeding

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

There is no firm evidence that gabapentin is harmful to an unborn baby but for safety women are usually advised to take it in pregnancy only if the benefits of the medicine outweigh the potential risks.

You can generally breastfeed as normal while taking gabapentin.

Tell your doctor immediately if you are trying to get pregnant or become pregnant while taking this medicine.

More information

Women who are trying to get pregnant or have become pregnant are routinely recommended to take at least 400mcg of a vitamin called folic acid everyday. It helps the unborn baby grow normally.

Gabapentin has been linked with low folic acid levels. So, if you take gabapentin, your doctor will prescribe a high dose of folic acid (5mg a day) for you to take during the first 12 weeks of pregnancy.

If you take gabapentin around the time of giving birth, your baby may need extra monitoring for a few days after they’re born because they may have gabapentin withdrawal symptoms.

Gabapentin and breastfeeding

Tiny amounts of gabapentin get into breast milk but generally, you can breastfeed as normal while taking it.

Check with your doctor first if your baby is premature or has kidney problems.

More about gabapentin

Gabapentin withdrawal

Don't stop taking gabapentin without talking to your doctor – you will need to reduce the dose gradually.

Do not stop taking gabapentin suddenly even if you feel fine. Gabapentin can build up in the body so that if you stop taking it suddenly you may have a severe withdrawal syndrome with unpleasant symptoms that include:

  • anxiety
  • difficulty sleeping
  • feeling sick
  • pain
  • sweating

It's possible to prevent withdrawal symptoms by reducing the dose bit by bit.

How gabapentin works

It’s not clear exactly how gabapentin works. It’s thought to interfere with pain messages travelling through the brain and down the spine to block pain.

Gabapentin and street drugs

Gabapentin can make you feel relaxed, calm and 'high'. It can also intensify the 'highs' of street drugs like cannabis and heroin. So, if you use street drugs alongside gabapentin, there may be more chance of unpleasant side effects like panic attacks, anxiety and memory loss.

Gabapentin and addiction

Some people have become addicted to gabapentin after taking it for a long time and have withdrawal symptoms after they stop taking the medicine. Talk to your doctor if you’re concerned you are becoming physically dependent on gabapentin.
