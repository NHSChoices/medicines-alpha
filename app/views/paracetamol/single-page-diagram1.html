Paracetamol is a widely used painkiller that is available over the counter without a prescription to treat aches and pain. It can also be used to reduce fever.

Paracetamol may also be called by a brand name such as Calpol, Disprol, Hedex, Medinol and Panadol.

It's also available combined with other painkillers and anti-sickness medicines and is an ingredient in a wide range of cold and flu remedies.

Key facts

  • Paracetamol takes up to an hour to work
  • The usual dose of paracetamol is one or two 500mg tablets
  • Never take more than eight 500mg tablets in 24 hours
  • Do not take paracetamol with other paracetamol-containing medicines
  • Paracetamol is generally safe to take in pregnancy and while breastfeeding at recommended doses
Info:

Check the leaflet inside your medicine packet for specific information about the brand that you are taking.

How much to take

  • adults can take ONE or TWO 500mg tablets or capsules at a time
  • up to a maximum of four times in 24 hours
  • space out doses at least four hours apart

For people who are in constant pain, paracetamol can be taken regularly up to four times in 24 hours with doses spread out as evenly as possible.

Paracetamol can also be present in other medicines. Make sure you do not take any other medicines containing paracetamol at the same time.

More information

You may be advised to take lower doses of paracetamol if you:

  • have liver or kidney problems
  • are very thin
  • regularly drink more than the recommended amount of alcohol

For children

The total maximum dose for children depends on the child's age. You can find out the correct dose on the packet. If you aren't sure check with your pharmacist or doctor.

If you're giving paracetamol to a child make sure you use the measuring spoon supplied with the medicine to correctly measure the amount of liquid.

What if I take too much?

An overdose of paracetamol can cause very serious and sometimes fatal liver problems.

Accidental overdose happens most often in people taking more than the daily recommended dose.

Even taking one or two more tablets than recommended can cause serious liver damage and possibly death.

Overdose can also happen if you are not aware you are taking more than one medicine with paracetamol in it.

Talk to your doctor or pharmacist if you are unsure whether any other medicine you are taking also contains paracetamol.

Early symptoms of paracetamol overdose are:

  • nausea and vomiting (which usually settles within 24 hours)
  • pain and tenderness on the right side below the ribs (which may not appear until three or four days after the overdose)

If you think you may have taken too much paracetamol, go straight to your nearest hospital A&E department.

What if I miss a prescribed dose?

If you take paracetamol regularly and miss a dose take it as soon as you remember. If it's close to the time for your next dose when you remember, then skip the missed dose.

Do not take double doses of paracetamol at the same time or extra doses to catch up.

Mixing paracetamol with other medicines

In general, paracetamol is not affected by other medicines.

It's important, though, that you do not take more than one medicine containing paracetamol at a time.

For safety, talk to your doctor or pharmacist before adding in another type of medicine including ones you buy like herbal remedies and supplements.

More information

Paracetamol and other paracetamol-containing medicines

Do not take paracetamol with other medicines that contain paracetamol.

Paracetamol is an ingredient in some over-the-counter remedies, including many cold and flu products.

Paracetamol may also be combined with other painkillers which you may have been prescribed by your doctor such as:

  • co-codamol (paracetamol and codeine)
  • co-dydramol (paracetamol and dihydrocodeine)
  • tramacet (paracetamol and tramadol)

Before taking any other medicines, check the label to see whether they contain paracetamol.

Paracetamol and other types of painkiller

You can take paracetamol with other types of painkiller (that don't contain paracetamol) such as ibuprofen and aspirin if your pain isn't controlled by paracetamol alone.

Paracetamol and prescription medicines

Talk to your doctor if you take:

  • warfarin
  • anti-epilepsy medicine
  • medicine to treat tuberculosis (TB)

Taking paracetamol regularly over a long period of time may increase the blood-thinning (anticoagulant) effect of warfarin which could increase the risk of bleeding.

If you take medicines for epilepsy or tuberculosis, you may need to take lower doses of paracetamol.

Side effects

Side effects from paracetamol are rare as long as you don't take more than the recommended amount.

Most people have no side effects. Very occasionally, you can get an upset stomach, bleeding problems or have an allergic reaction.

More information

Side effects that can come on gradually

  • upset stomach or vomiting
  • bruising or bleeding - rarely paracetamol may cause your blood to clot less well.
  • infections - rarely paracetamol can reduce the number of your white blood cells. This makes you more likely to get infections.

Serious side effects

It happens very rarely, but you can get very serious side effects from taking paracetamol.

Stop taking paracetamol and contact a doctor URGENTLY if you have any of the following side effects while taking paracetamol:

  • An allergic reaction: warning signs include rash; itching or swelling (especially of the face, tongue or throat); trouble breathing or talking.
  • Liver problems: warning signs include dark urine, feeling tired, not hungry, upset stomach or stomach pain, light-coloured poo, vomiting, or yellow skin or eyes.
  • Kidney problems: warning signs are not being able to pass urine or a change in how much urine you pass.
  • Skin reactions: A very serious skin reaction called Stevens-Johnson syndrome can happen very occasionally. Warning signs include red, swollen, blistered, or peeling skin; red or irritated eyes; or sores in your mouth, throat, nose, or eyes.
Info:

These are not all the side effects of paracetamol. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicines packet.

Common questions

Can I drink alcohol with paracetamol?

It's advised that you cut down or stop drinking alcohol completely while you take paracetamol.

Can I take ibuprofen with paracetamol?

Yes, you can take ibuprofen with paracetamol.

Can I take paracetamol while I'm pregnant or breastfeeding?

Yes. Paracetamol is considered safe to take in pregnancy and while breastfeeding at the recommended doses.

Can I take complementary remedies with paracetamol?

Check with your doctor or pharmacist if you're taking the herbal medicine St John's Wort for depression as you may need to reduce your paracetamol dose.

Otherwise, paracetamol is not generally affected by also taking complementary medicines.

Are there any foods I should avoid?

No. Paracetamol is not affected by any foods.

Who can take paracetamol

Paracetamol needs to be taken with extra care by some people.

Check with your doctor or pharmacist what dose of paracetamol is safe if you:

  • are very thin
  • have a history of liver or kidney problems
  • regularly drink more than the maximum recommended amount
  • take medicine for epilepsy
  • take medicine for tuberculosis (TB)

More about paracetamol

How paracetamol works

It's not fully understood how paracetamol works but it's thought to interfere with naturally occurring hormones called prostaglandins which are important in changing the pain threshold and reducing fever.

When will I feel better?

Paracetamol takes up to an hour to work as a painkiller and to bring down a high temperature.

Long-term dangers

Research has suggested that if you take paracetamol regularly for many years you are slightly more likely to have a heart attack, bleeding inside the digestive system or kidney problems.

But it isn't clear whether the increased risk is due to other health or lifestyle differences rather than long-term use of paracetamol.

Other treatments

There are a number of different types of painkiller that you can use instead of paracetamol including:

  • ibuprofen
  • codeine

Ibuprofen and ibuprofen-like painkillers are sometimes available as creams or gels that you rub onto a specific part of the body that is painful.

Some painkillers are only available by prescription so talk to your doctor or pharmacist about the other treatment options that might be more suitable for you.
