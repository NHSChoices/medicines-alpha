Atorvastatin belongs to a group of medicines called statins. It’s often used to lower the levels of cholesterol and other fatty substances (triglycerides) in the blood.

It is also taken by people who have had a heart attack or who have unstable angina to reduce the risk of another heart attack or stroke.

If you are at high risk of developing cardiovascular disease, for example, because you have diabetes, atorvastatin also helps reduce this risk.

Atorvastatin can be taken by children over the age of 10.

The medicine is available as tablets on prescription only.

Atorvastatin is also called by the brand name of Lipitor.

Key facts

  • Atorvastatin is usually taken once a day.
  • The most common side effects are headaches, feeling sick, diarrhoea and cold-like symptoms.
  • Atorvastatin can harm a baby. Tell your doctor if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant or breastfeeding.
  • Don’t drink more than one or two glasses of grapefruit juice a day while taking atorvastatin as it increases the chances of side effects.

How and when to take

Take atorvastatin once a day. You can choose to take it at any time, as long as you stick to the same time every day.

Atorvastatin doesn't upset the stomach so you can take it with or without food.

The usual dose is between 10mg and 80mg a day.

More information

Swallow atorvastatin tablets whole with a glass of water. If you have been given chewable tablets, you can chew them or swallow them whole with a glass of water.

How much will I take?

Your dose depends on the reason for taking it, your cholesterol levels and what other medicines you are taking.

In adults, the usual dose is 10mg to 80mg once a day.

In children, the usual dose is 10mg to 20mg once a day. Sometimes higher doses are used - your doctor will work out the amount of atorvastatin that is right for your child.

What if I forget to take it?

If you forget to take a dose, take your next dose the next day at the usual time. Never take two doses at the same time or extra doses.

What if I take too much?

Accidentally taking an extra dose of atorvastatin is unlikely to harm you.

Speak to your pharmacist or doctor if you are worried, or take more than one extra dose.

Side effects

Not everyone will get side effects with atorvastatin but common ones include feeling sick, headache, back and joint pain and nosebleeds.

One rare but serious side effect is unexplained muscle pain, tenderness or weakness.

Report any unexplained muscle pain, tenderness or weakness to a doctor straight away.

More information

Common side effects

Like all medicines, atorvastatin can cause side effects in some people.

These common side effects of atorvastatin happen in fewer that one in 10 people. Keep taking the medicine, but tell your doctor if they bother you or don’t go away:

  • feeling sick
  • headache
  • pain in the back and joints
  • nosebleeds
  • sore throat
  • cold like symptoms such as runny nose, blocked nose or sneezing
  • digestive complaints, such as constipation, wind, indigestion or diarrhoea

Serious side effects

Serious side effects are rare when taking atorvastatin.

Stop taking atorvastatin and tell a doctor or go to hospital straight away if you get:

  • muscle pain, tenderness, weakness or cramps (a sign of muscle breakdown and kidney damage)
  • liver problems: warning signs include yellowing of the skin and eyes, dark-coloured urine, pale coloured poo, itching, feeling tired or weak and loss of appetite
  • skin rash with pink-red blotches which may blister especially on palms of hands or soles of feet
  • severe tummy pain (a sign of inflammation of the pancreas)
  • cough, feeling short of breath and weight loss (signs of interstitial lung disease)
Info:

These are not all the side effects of atorvastatin. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.

Serious allergic reaction

It's rare to have a serious allergic reaction to atorvastatin but if this happens to you, stop taking the medicine and contact a doctor straight away.

The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

  • a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
  • wheezing
  • tightness in the chest or throat
  • trouble breathing or talking
  • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat

Serious allergic reaction is a medical emergency. Contact a doctor straight away if you think you or someone around you is having a serious allergic reaction.

Info:

If you have a side effect from any medicine, including atorvastatin, you can report it using the Yellow Card Scheme.

Who can take atorvastatin

Atorvastatin isn’t suitable for some people.

Tell your doctor if you:

  • have an allergy or intolerance to atorvastatin
  • have liver or kidney problems
  • are trying for a baby or not using contraception, pregnant or breastfeeding
  • have severe lung disease
  • have previously had a stroke caused by bleeding into the brain
  • drink large amounts of alcohol (more than two glasses a day)
  • have an underactive thyroid
  • have had muscular side effects when taking a statin in the past
  • (or a close relative) have had a muscle disorder

Lipitor chewable tablets contain a substance called aspartame - check with your doctor before taking these if you have phenylketonuria (an inherited disorder of protein metabolism).

Common questions

Can I drink alcohol?

Yes, you can drink while taking atorvastatin.

However, drinking a lot of alcohol may increase the risk of muscle and liver side effects. Try not to drink more than two glasses of alcohol a day.

How long will I take atorvastatin for?

In most cases, treatment with a statin such as atorvastatin is for life. The benefits will only continue for as long as you take it. If you stop taking atorvastatin without starting a different treatment your cholesterol level may rise again.

Can I drive while taking atorvastatin?

Yes – atorvastatin is unlikely to affect your ability to drive.

Will it stop my contraception working?

For women, atorvastatin may slightly increase the hormones released into your system from some contraceptive pills. You will still be protected from pregnancy but this could increase your chances of side effects from contraceptive pills.

If you have any side effects, talk to your doctor or pharmacist.

Will it reduce my fertility?

There is no evidence atorvastatin will reduce your fertility.

Is atorvastatin addictive?

No, there is no evidence that atorvastatin is addictive.

Taking atorvastatin with other medicines

Some medicines affect the way atorvastatin works, and can increase the risk of serious side effects such as muscle damage.

Tell your doctor and pharmacist if you are taking any other medicines, including herbal remedies or supplements, before starting atorvastatin.

More information

Medicines that may not mix well with atorvastatin include:

  • some antibiotics and antifungals
  • some HIV medicines
  • some hepatitis C medicines
  • warfarin (stops blood clotting)
  • ciclosporin (treats psoriasis and rheumatoid arthritis)
  • colchicine (a medicine for gout)
  • contraceptive pills
  • verapamil, diltiazem, amlodipine (for high blood pressure and heart problems)
  • amiodarone (makes your heart stable)

If you are taking atorvastatin and need to take one of these medicines, your doctor may:

  • prescribe a lower dose of atorvastatin
  • prescribe a different statin medicine
  • recommend that you temporarily stop taking your atorvastatin

Mixing atorvastatin with complementary remedies

St John’s Wort, a herbal medicine taken for depression, reduces the amount of atorvastatin in your blood so it doesn't work as well.

Talk to your doctor before starting St John’s Wort as you may need a higher dose of atorvastatin.

Tell your doctor and pharmacist that you are taking atorvastatin before starting or stopping any complementary medicine.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

Atorvastatin may harm a baby. Do not take it if you are pregnant or breastfeeding.

If you become pregnant while taking atorvastatin, stop taking the medicine and tell your doctor.

Tell your doctor if you plan to get pregnant, become pregnant, or wish to breastfeed while taking this medicine.

More information

It's not recommended that women take atorvastatin in pregnancy as there is no firm evidence that it is safe.

Talk to your doctor if you want to try for a baby. It's best to stop taking atorvastatin at least three months before you start trying for a baby.

Atorvastatin and breastfeeding

It’s not known if atorvastatin passes into breast milk but it may cause problems for your baby.

Talk to your doctor if you want to breastfeed. You may be able to stop atorvastatin temporarily while you breastfeed.

More about atorvastatin

How atorvastatin works

Atorvastatin acts on the liver to stop it making cholesterol. This lowers your overall blood cholesterol level.

Are there other cholesterol-lowering medicines?

There are several other cholesterol-lowering 'statin' medicines including:

  • simvastatin
  • fluvastatin
  • pravastatin
  • rosuvastatin

Statins all work in the same way, but they differ in how well they lower cholesterol.

If you have a side effect with one statin, this may not happen with another. And some medicines don’t mix well with one statin but you can take them with a different one.

Your doctor will find the right statin and dose for you, depending on your medical history, cholesterol level and the other medicines you take.

Food and drinks to avoid

Don't drink more than one or two small glasses of grapefruit juice a day if you are taking atorvastatin.

Large quantities of grapefruit juice increase the concentration of atorvastatin in your blood which makes side effects more likely.

Coming off atorvastatin

Talk to your doctor if you stop or want to stop taking atorvastatin.

You may want to stop atorvastatin if you think you are having side effects. Talk to your doctor to see if it really is a side effect of atorvastatin or another problem. It may help to lower your dose or change your medicine.

Stopping atorvastatin may cause your cholesterol to rise. If you want to stop taking the medicine, it’s important to find another way to lower your cholesterol.

Non-medical treatment options

There are lifestyle changes you can make that will help lower your cholesterol. Reducing your cholesterol may mean you don't need to be prescribed a statin medicine.

  • Lose excess weight.
  • Eat heart-healthy foods by decreasing saturated fat and increasing fibre, fruits, vegetables, whole grains and fish.
  • Stop smoking.
  • Reduce the amount of alcohol you drink.
  • Exercise every day

As part of a healthy diet, it can also help to include foods fortified with plant sterols and stanols. They are proven to help lower cholesterol. You can buy spreads, cheese, cereals, granola bars, orange juice, wholemeal bread and yoghurt-style drinks which have been fortified with plant sterols and stanols from most supermarkets.
