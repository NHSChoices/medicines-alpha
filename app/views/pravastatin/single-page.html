Pravastatin belongs to a group of medicines called statins.

It’s often used to lower the levels of cholesterol and other fatty substances (triglycerides) in your blood.

It is also taken by people who have had a heart attack or who have unstable angina to reduce the risk of another heart attack or stroke.

If you are at high risk of developing cardiovascular disease, for example, because you have diabetes, pravastatin also helps reduce this risk.

The medicine is available as tablets on prescription only.

Key facts

  • Take pravastatin once a day in the evening.
  • Pravastatin can harm a baby. Tell your doctor if you are pregnant, planning to become pregnant or breastfeeding.
  • Take pravastatin even if you feel well as you will still be getting the benefits. Most people with high cholesterol don’t have any symptoms.
  • You can drink grapefruit juice with pravastatin.
Info:

Check the leaflet inside your medicine packet for specific information about the brand that you are taking.

How and when to take

Take pravastatin once a day in the evening.

In adults, the dose may range from 10mg to 40mg daily. Your dose depends on the reason for taking it, your cholesterol levels and what other medicines you are taking.

More information

Swallow the tablets whole with a glass of water. You can take pravastatin with or without food.

Pravastatin may be taken by children aged 8 to 17 years old with high cholesterol or high triglycerides (fatty substances in the blood).

In children aged 8 to 13 years old the usual dose range is 10mg to 20mg daily. In children aged 14 to 17 years old, the dose may range from 10mg to 40mg daily. Your doctor will work out the amount of pravastatin that is right for your child.

What if I forget to take it?

If you forget to take a dose, take your next dose the next day at the usual time. Never take two doses at the same time or extra doses.

What if I take too much?

Accidentally taking an extra dose of pravastatin is unlikely to harm you.

Call NHS 111 or speak to your pharmacist or doctor if you are worried, or take more than one extra dose.

Side effects

Pravastatin seems to be a very safe medicine. It is rare to have side effects with it.

One rare but serious side effect is unexplained muscle pain, tenderness or weakness. Muscle symptoms can indicate muscle breakdown and kidney damage.

Report any unexplained muscle pain, tenderness or weakness to a doctor straight away.

More information

Serious side effects

It happens rarely but up to one in 1000 people taking pravastatin may have a serious side effect.

Stop taking pravastatin and tell a doctor or go to hospital straight away if you develop:

  • unexplained muscle pain, tenderness, weakness or cramps
  • liver problems: warning signs include yellowing of the skin and eyes, dark-coloured urine, pale coloured poo, itching, feeling tired or weak and loss of appetite
  • severe tummy pain (a sign of inflammation of the pancreas)
  • cough, feeling short of breath and weight loss (signs of interstitial lung disease)

Serious allergic reaction

It's very rare to have an allergic reaction to pravastatin but if this happens to you, stop taking the medicine and contact a doctor straight away.

The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

  • getting a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
  • wheezing
  • tightness in the chest or throat
  • trouble breathing or talking
  • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat

Serious allergic reaction is a medical emergency. Contact a doctor straight away if you think you or someone around you is having a serious allergic reaction.

Info:

These are not all the side effects of pravastatin. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.

If you have a side effect from any medicine, including pravastatin, you can report it using the Yellow Card Scheme.

Who may not be able to take pravastatin

Pravastatin isn’t suitable for some people.

Tell your doctor if you:

  • have an allergy or intolerance to pravastatin
  • have liver or kidney problems
  • are trying for a baby or not using contraception, pregnant or breastfeeding
  • have severe lung disease
  • drink large amounts of alcohol (more than two glasses a day)
  • have an underactive thyroid
  • have had muscular side effects when taking a statin
  • (or a close relative) have had a muscle disorder

Common questions

Can I drink alcohol?

Yes, you can drink alcohol while taking pravastatin.

Drinking a lot of alcohol may increase the risk of muscle and liver side effects with pravastatin so it’s best not to drink more than two glasses a day.

How long will I take pravastatin for?

In most cases, treatment with a statin, such as pravastatin, is for life. The benefits will only continue for as long as you take it. If you stop taking pravastatin without starting a different treatment your cholesterol level may rise again.

Can I drive while taking pravastatin?

Most people can drive as normal while taking pravastatin. Occasionally, pravastatin can cause dizziness and blurred or double vision.

Don't drive or use machines or tools if you are suffering from dizziness or blurred or double vision.

Will it affect my contraception?

For women, pravastatin will not affect contraceptive pills or the morning after pill.

Will it affect my fertility?

There is no information about the effects of pravastatin on the fertility of men or women. If you are trying for a baby, it is important to discuss this with your doctor.

If you want to become pregnant, it's best to stop taking pravastatin three months before trying to conceive.

Is this medicine addictive?

No, there is no evidence that pravastatin is addictive.

Taking pravastatin with other medicines

Tell your doctor and pharmacist that you are taking pravastatin before starting or stopping any other prescription or medicine you buy.

Some medicines affect the way pravastatin works, and can increase the risk of serious side effects with pravastatin, including muscle damage. Pravastatin can also affect the way some medicines work.

If you are taking pravastatin and need to take one of these medicines, your doctor may:

  • prescribe a lower dose of pravastatin
  • recommend that you temporarily stop taking your pravastatin
  • advise increased monitoring
More information

Examples of medicines that may not mix well with pravastatin:

  • some antibiotics and antifungals
  • some HIV medicines
  • some hepatitis C medicines
  • ciclosporin (treats psoriasis and rheumatoid arthritis)
  • colchicine (a medicine for gout)
  • medicines to reduce cholesterol and fatty substances in the blood e.g. fibrates, colestipol
Info:

For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet, or talk to your doctor or pharmacist.

Mixing pravastatin with complementary remedies

Tell your doctor and pharmacist that you are taking pravastatin before starting or stopping any complementary medicine. They will be able to check if there are any known problems.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

There is no evidence that pravastatin can harm an unborn baby but to be safe it's best not to take it if you become pregnant.

It's also best to stop taking pravastatin three months before trying to get pregnant.

Tell your doctor if you plan to get pregnant, become pregnant, or wish to breastfeed while taking this medicine.

More information

Pravastatin and breastfeeding

It's not known if pravastatin passes into breast milk but it may causes problems for your baby.

Talk to your doctor, if you are breastfeeding, or wish to breastfeed, and you take pravastatin. You may be able to stop taking the medicine just while you breastfeed.

More about pravastatin

How pravastatin works

Pravastatin blocks the enzyme (a natural protein) in your liver that helps to make cholesterol. This lowers your blood cholesterol level.

There are two types of cholesterol:

  • bad cholesterol is called low-density lipoprotein (LDL)
  • good cholesterol is called high-density lipoprotein (HDL)

Too much bad cholesterol can lead to fatty deposits building up in your arteries which can cause heart attacks and strokes.

Pravastatin reduces the production of bad cholesterol and other bad fats in the blood (triglycerides). It also increases the amount of good cholesterol.

What are the differences between statins?

Other statin medicines include:

  • simvastatin
  • fluvastatin
  • atorvastatin
  • rosuvastatin

Every statin can differ in the amount they lower cholesterol. For example, 20mg of atorvastatin causes a bigger reduction in bad cholesterol than 20mg of simvastatin.

If you have a side effect with one statin, this may not happen with another. Some medicines do not mix well with one statin but you can take them with a different statin.

Your doctor will find the right statin and dose for you, depending on your medical history, cholesterol and other medicines.

Coming off pravastatin

Talk to your doctor if you stop or want to stop taking pravastatin.

You may want to stop pravastatin if you think you are having side effects. Talk to your doctor to see if it is a side effect of pravastatin or another problem. It may help to lower your dose or change your medicine.

Stopping pravastatin may cause your cholesterol to rise, this may increase your risk of heart attacks and strokes. If you want to stop taking the medicine, it's important to find another way to lower your cholesterol and reduce the risk of cardiovascular events.

Non-medical treatment options

There are lifestyle changes you can make that will help lower your cholesterol. Reducing your cholesterol may mean you don't need to be prescribed a statin medicine.

  • Lose excess weight.
  • Eat heart-healthy foods by decreasing saturated fat and increasing fibre, fruits, vegetables, whole grains and fish.
  • Stop smoking.
  • Reduce the amount of alcohol you drink.
  • Exercise every day – start by taking a short walk.

There are also foods specially designed to lower your cholesterol, including certain dairy spreads and yoghurts containing added ingredients called plant sterols and stanols.
