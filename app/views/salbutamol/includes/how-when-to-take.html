How and when to take

Use your salbutamol inhaler as soon as you notice breathing symptoms, such as coughing, wheezing, shortness of breath and tightness in the chest. You should feel a difference to your breathing within a few minutes.

The normal way for adults and children to use their inhaler is:

  • 1 or 2 puffs of salbutamol when you need it
  • up to a maximum of 4 times in 24 hours (regardless of whether you have 1 puff or 2 puffs at a time)

Salbutamol is sometimes prescribed to prevent breathing symptoms happening in the first place. This could be before a trigger such as exercise or pets. In this situation, the normal dose is still 1 or 2 puffs at a time.

In a sudden, serious asthma attack you can take a larger dose of salbutamol of up to 10 puffs (waiting 15 to 30 seconds between puffs). You can repeat this dose 10 minutes later.

Asthma attacks can get worse very quickly. If you or your child are struggling to breathe or have asthma symptoms that are not getting better, go to hospital immediately or call an ambulance.

If you need to use your inhaler more than 4 times in 24 hours:

  • it probably means that your health problem is getting worse and that you may need additional treatment.
  • you are more likely to get side effects such as increased heart rate, jitteriness, nervousness and headaches

Make an appointment to see your doctor or nurse if you need:

  • to use your inhaler more than 4 times in 24 hours
  • more than 8 puffs of your inhaler in 24 hours
  • to use your inhaler on more than 2 days of each week
  • to use your inhaler in the middle of the night at least once a week

What if I take too much?

You may notice that your heart beats more quickly than normal and that you feel shaky. These side effects usually pass within a few minutes or a few hours at most and are not dangerous.

Serious asthma attacks

Salbutamol can be given through a nebuliser to treat severe asthma attacks. A nebuliser is a machine that delivers the medicine as a mist inhaled through a face mask.

Salbutamol tablets and syrup

Salbutamol tablets or syrup don't work as quickly as an inhaler so you need to take them regularly to keep your airways open.
