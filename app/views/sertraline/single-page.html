Sertraline is a type of antidepressant that is often used to treat depression, and also sometimes panic attacks or obsessive compulsive disorder.

Sertraline helps many people recover from depression, and it has fewer unwanted effects than older antidepressants.

It is also called Lustral.

Key facts

  • It usually takes 4-6 weeks to feel the full benefits of sertraline.
  • Side effects such as feeling sick, headache and trouble sleeping are common, but tend to be mild and pass after a couple of weeks.
  • Sertraline can affect an unborn baby. Tell your doctor straight away if you’re trying to get pregnant or become pregnant while taking it.
  • Sertraline can cause withdrawal symptoms so don’t stop taking it without talking to your doctor.
  • You can drink alcohol while taking sertraline but it might make you feel sleepy.

How and when to take

Take sertraline once a day. You can choose to take it at any time, as long as you stick to the same time every day. You can take sertraline with or without food.

For depression and obsessive compulsive disorder, the usual dose of sertraline in adults is 50mg daily. For panic attacks, the usual starting dose of sertraline in adults is 25mg daily for the first week, then 50mg daily after that.

More information

Sertraline can be used by children aged 6-17 years old, but only for obsessive compulsive disorder.

The usual starting dose in children aged 6-12 years is 25mg daily but your child’s doctor might increase this to 50mg daily after one week, if needed. The usual starting dose in children aged 13-17 years is 50mg daily.

Will my dose go up or down?

The usual dose of sertraline is 50mg daily in adults. However, it might be started at a lower dose and may be increased gradually to a maximum dose of 200mg daily.

If you have liver problems your doctor might give you a lower dose, or advise you to take sertraline less often.

The usual dose of sertraline in children aged 6-12 is 25mg daily, but this may be increased to 50mg daily after one week. The usual dose of sertraline in children aged 13-17 is 50mg daily. Children of any age might have their dose increased up to 200mg daily, if needed.

What if I forget to take it?

If you forget to take a dose, don’t worry. Take your next dose the next day at the usual time.

What if I take too much?

Ask your doctor for advice straight away as overdose can lead to potentially serious symptoms such as:

  • vomiting
  • tremor
  • feeling sleepy
  • dizziness
  • fast heart rate
  • seizures

Never take two doses at the same time or extra doses.

Side effects

Not everyone will get side effects with sertraline, but common ones include feeling sick, headache, sleep problems, and diarrhoea.

More information

Common side effects

Like all medicines, sertraline can cause side effects in some people but many people have no side effects or only minor ones.

These very common side effects affect more than one in 10 people. Keep taking the medicine, but tell your doctor if they bother you or don't go away:

  • feeling sick
  • headache
  • being unable to sleep
  • feeling sleepy
  • diarrhoea
  • dry mouth
  • dizziness
  • feeling tired or weak
  • in men, problems ejaculating

Serious side effects

It happens rarely, but some people may have serious side effects when taking sertraline.

Tell a doctor straight away if you develop:

  • serotonin syndrome: warning signs include dizziness, very severe headache, agitation, hallucinations, fever, fast or unusual heartbeat, flushing, seizures, shakiness, sweating a lot, not thinking clearly, very upset stomach and vomiting, or severe diarrhoea
  • low sodium levels: warning signs include headache, trouble focusing, memory problems, not thinking clearly, weakness, seizures, or losing your balance
  • thoughts about harming yourself or ending your life
  • chest pain or pressure or shortness of breath
  • severe dizziness or passing out
  • fits, feelings of euphoria, excessive enthusiasm or excitement, or a feeling of restlessness that means you can’t sit or stand still
  • yellow skin or eyes
  • putting on or losing weight without trying to
  • changes in your periods such as heavy bleeding, spotting, or bleeding between periods
  • painful erections that last longer than four hours - this may happen even when you are not having sex

Or, if you get any signs of abnormal bleeding including:

  • bleeding from the gut: warning signs include vomiting blood or dark vomit; coughing up blood; blood in your urine; black or red poo
  • bleeding from the gums or bruises that appear without a reason or that get bigger
  • any bleeding that is very bad or that you cannot stop

Serious allergic reaction

It’s rare to have an allergic reaction to sertraline but if this happens to you, stop taking the medicine and contact a doctor straight away.

The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

  • getting a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
  • wheezing
  • tightness in the chest or throat
  • having trouble breathing or talking
  • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat
Info:

These are not all the side effects of sertraline. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.

Coping with side effects

Some of the common side effects of sertraline will gradually improve as your body gets used to it.

  • What to do about nausea or indigestion: take sertraline with food and plenty of water.
  • What to do about feeling sleepy: avoid driving or using tools or machinery until the sleepiness wears off. Reduce the amount of alcohol you drink.
  • What to do if you’re unable to sleep: take sertraline first thing in the morning
  • What to do if you have a dry mouth: chew sugar-free gum or sugar-free sweets
  • What to do if you feel anxious: some people who take sertraline for panic attacks find their anxiety worsens during the first few weeks of treatment. This usually wears off after a few weeks but speak to your doctor if it bothers you - a lower dose may help reduce your symptoms.

You can lessen the chance of having a side effect if you take sertraline in the evening so that you're asleep when the level of medicine in your body is highest.

If you have diabetes sertraline can upset its control. Monitor your blood glucose more often for the first few weeks of treatment with sertraline and adjust your diabetes treatment if necessary.

If you have a side effect from any medicine, including sertraline, you can report it using the Yellow Card Scheme.

Who may not be able to take fluoxetine

Sertraline may not be suitable for everyone. Check with your doctor before starting to take sertraline if you:

  • have an allergy to sertraline or any of its ingredients
  • are taking any other medicines, including any complementary medicines or medicines that affect your heartbeat
  • have ever taken any other medicines for depression (some rarely used antidepressants can interact with sertraline to cause very high blood pressure even when they have been stopped for a few weeks)
  • have ever been diagnosed with schizophrenia or with any disease that causes fits, like epilepsy
  • are trying to become pregnant, are already pregnant or breastfeeding

Common questions

Is sertraline better than other antidepressants?

Sertraline isn't any better or worse than other antidepressants. Sometimes people respond better to one antidepressant than another. If you aren’t feeling any better after 6 weeks, talk to your doctor.

Can I drink alcohol?

You can drink alcohol while taking sertraline but you might find it makes you more sleepy, or makes you lose your focus. During the first few days of taking sertraline, it might be best to stop drinking alcohol until you see how the medicine affects you.

Will I gain or lose weight?

Sertraline can make you feel more or less hungry than usual, so you may lose or gain weight when you start taking it.

If you start to have problems with your weight while taking sertraline, talk to your doctor or pharmacist.

Will it affect my sex life?

The good effects of sertraline may, after a while, enhance your sex life as your mood lifts and you become interested in life and relationships again.

Some of the possible negative effects include:

  • men might get painful erections, problems with getting an erection and problems with ejaculating
  • women might have some vaginal bleeding and might not reach orgasm the same way as before
  • you may have a lower sex drive

Sexual side effects should pass after the first couple of weeks. If they don’t, and this a problem for you, go back to your doctor to see if there’s another antidepressant for you to try.

Will street drugs affect it?

It can be potentially dangerous (even fatal) to take sertraline with:

  • stimulants like MDMA (ecstasy) or cocaine
  • hallucinogens like LSD
  • legal highs like mephedrone

The sleep-inducing effects of cannabis can add to those of sertraline, especially in people who have just started taking it.

Methadone can increase the risk of side effects in people taking sertraline.

Sertraline has not been properly tested with any street drugs so tell your doctor if think you might use any street drugs while taking sertraline.

Can I drive or ride a bike?

Most people drive as normal while taking sertraline. However, it might affect your concentration to do things like driving a car, riding a bike or anything else that needs a lot of focus. It might be best to stop doing these things for the first few days until you know how it affects you.

Will it affect my contraception?

For women, sertraline will not affect contraceptive pills or the morning after pill.

Taking sertraline with other medicines

Tell your doctor and pharmacist that you are taking sertraline before starting or stopping any other prescription medicine or one you buy from a pharmacy or shop.

More information

Mixing sertraline with complementary remedies

Don't take the herbal remedy for depression, St John's wort, at the same time as sertraline as this will increase your risk of side effects.

Tell your doctor and pharmacist that you're taking sertraline before starting any complementary medicine.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding

No antidepressants are considered completely safe to take in pregnancy or while breastfeeding, but your doctor will want to prescribe sertraline when the benefits to you outweigh the risks.

More information

Taking sertraline in pregnancy

Sertraline and similar antidepressants have occasionally been linked with a small risk of problems for the unborn baby when they're taken in early or late pregnancy.

Tell your doctor if you’re trying to get pregnant or become pregnant while taking sertraline.

Taking sertraline while breastfeeding

Sertraline passes into breast milk and has been linked with side effects in breastfed babies.

Talk to your doctor about taking sertraline while you are breastfeeding.

More about sertraline

How sertraline works

Sertraline is one of a group of antidepressants called selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors, or SSRIs, which are thought to work by increasing the levels of a mood-enhancing chemical called serotonin in the brain.

When will I feel better?

You may see an improvement in your symptoms after a week or two although it usually takes between 4 and 6 weeks before you feel the full benefits. That’s because it takes around a week for sertraline levels to build up in your body, and then a few weeks longer for your body to adapt and get used to it.

Don't stop taking sertraline after a week or two just because you feel it is not helping your symptoms. Give the medicine at least 6 weeks to work.

Food and drinks to avoid

It's best not to drink grapefruit juice while you're taking this medicine. Grapefruit juice can increase the amount of sertraline in your body and increase the risk of side effects.

How long will I take sertraline for?

Once your condition is under control it is likely that you will continue to take sertraline for several more months to prevent the symptoms returning.

You will need to discuss with your doctor the pros and cons of continuing to take sertraline in the long term. The decision will depend on the type and severity of your initial symptoms, whether it's a one-off or recurring problem, how well sertraline works for you, and whether you've had any serious side effects.

What else can sertraline be used for?

Sertraline is mainly used to treat depression, panic attacks, social anxiety, obsessive compulsive disorder, and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). It is also occasionally used to treat generalised anxiety disorder.

Sertraline is sometimes prescribed to treat depression in teenage children aged 12-17 years old.

Coming off sertraline

Don't stop taking sertraline unless your doctor tells you to.

If you stop taking sertraline suddenly you may get unpleasant withdrawal symptoms. These include:

  • dizziness
  • feeling sick
  • numbness or tingling in the hands or feet
  • feeling anxious
  • headaches
  • shaking

Withdrawal symptoms are usually harmless and over within a few days but it's possible to prevent them by reducing the dose gradually when you stop taking this medicine.

Non-medical treatment options

Antidepressants, including sertraline, are just one of several approaches to treating depression. Other potential treatments include:

  • talking therapy (such as cognitive behavioural therapy)
  • exercise programmes
  • help to get a good night's sleep

Choosing a treatment that's most suitable for you depends on how long you have had depression, your symptoms, whether you have had any previous bouts of depression, whether previous treatment has worked, how likely you are to stick with your treatment, the potential side effects and your preferences and priorities.
