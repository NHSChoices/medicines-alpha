Side effects

Like all medicines, gliclazide can cause side effects although not everyone gets them.

Common side effects

These common side effects happen in up to one in 10 people:

  • stomach ache
  • feeling sick
  • vomiting
  • indigestion
  • diarrhoea
  • constipation

Side effects can be less likely if you take gliclazide tablets before a meal.

Serious side effects

These serious side effects are rare and affect less than one in 10,000 people.

Tell your doctor straight away if you get:

  • liver disorder - warning signs include yellowing of the skin or whites of the eye
  • blood disorder - warning signs include paleness, prolonged bleeding, bruising, sore throat and fever
  • skin disorders such as rash, redness, itching, hives - you can also have rapid swelling of eyelids, face, lips, mouth, tongue or throat that may result in breathing difficulty
  • eye disorders - your vision may be affected for a short time especially at the start of treatment because of changes in your blood sugar levels

Low blood sugar

Gliclazide can sometimes cause your blood sugar to go too low. The name for this is hypoglycaemia. Early warning signs of low blood sugar include:

  • feeling hungry
  • trembling or shaking
  • sweating
  • confusion
  • difficulty concentrating

It’s also possible for your blood sugar to go too low while you’re asleep. If this happens, it can make you feel sweaty, tired and confused when you wake up.

Low blood sugar may happen if you:

  • eat meals irregularly or skip meals
  • are fasting
  • don’t eat a healthy diet with enough nutrients in
  • change what you eat
  • increase your physical activity without eating more to compensate
  • drink alcohol, especially after skipping a meal
  • take other medicines or natural remedies at the same time
  • take too much gliclazide
  • suffer from a hormone disorder such as hypothyroidism
  • have kidney or liver problems

Have regular meals, including breakfast, and never miss or delay a meal.

If you are planning to increase your physical activity, eat carbohydrates (bread, pasta, cereals) before, during or after exercise.

Always carry a fast-acting carbohydrate with you, like sugar cubes, fruit juice, or some sweets in case your blood sugar level gets low. Artificial sweeteners won’t help. You may also need to eat a starchy carbohydrate, like a sandwich or a biscuit, to maintain your blood sugar for longer

If taking sugar does not help or if the symptoms come back, contact your doctor or the nearest hospital.

Make sure your friends and family know about your diabetes and the symptoms of low blood sugar level.

These are not all the side effects of gliclazide. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.
